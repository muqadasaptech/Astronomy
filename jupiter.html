Jupiter

The solar system has eight planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.
There are five officially recognized dwarf planets in our solar system: Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris.

Introduction

Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, is a gas giant known for its prominent bands and the Great Red Spot—a massive storm persisting for centuries. With its powerful gravitational field, Jupiter has over 79 known moons and is mostly composed of hydrogen and helium, resembling a small star in composition.

Size

Jupiter's diameter is about 143,000 kilometers (89,000 miles), making it more than 11 times wider than Earth. This immense size allows it to influence other celestial bodies and stabilize the orbits of many nearby asteroids.

Temperature

Jupiter's temperature varies widely: the upper atmosphere averages around -145°C (-234°F), but the core, heated by intense pressure, reaches up to 24,000°C (43,000°F). The planet emits more heat than it receives from the Sun, hinting at intense internal processes.

Ring

Jupiter’s ring system, discovered by the Voyager 1 spacecraft, is faint and consists mainly of dust particles from its moons. Unlike Saturn’s, these rings are thin and nearly invisible, but they span great distances around the planet.

Namesake

Jupiter is named after the Roman king of gods, symbolizing strength and dominance. The name reflects the planet's majestic size and influence within our solar system.

Distance

Jupiter orbits the Sun at an average distance of about 778 million kilometers (484 million miles). Its vast distance from the Sun positions it beyond the asteroid belt, nearly five times farther than Earth.

Formation

Formed around 4.5 billion years ago, Jupiter likely emerged early in the solar system’s history, accumulating gas and dust from the protoplanetary disk. Its rapid formation played a role in shaping the structure of the outer solar system.

Atmosphere

Jupiter’s atmosphere comprises mostly hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane, ammonia, and water vapor. Its iconic bands result from rapid rotation and complex weather systems. Strong winds and colorful clouds create its striking striped appearance.

Surface

While Jupiter lacks a solid surface, its upper atmosphere gradually transitions to liquid hydrogen under extreme pressure. The intense atmospheric layers contain ammonia clouds, causing variations in color and cloud depth.

Structure

Jupiter is structured with an outer gaseous layer, followed by a layer of liquid metallic hydrogen that generates its strong magnetic field. At its core, Jupiter likely has a dense, rocky core about the size of Earth, though details remain uncertain due to extreme pressures.
