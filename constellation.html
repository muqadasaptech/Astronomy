Constellations

Constellations are groupings of stars that have been recognized and named by humans throughout history. They are often seen as patterns or pictures in the night sky, and different cultures have their own interpretations of these star patterns. The word "constellation" comes from the Latin "constellatio," meaning "set of stars." There are 88 officially recognized constellations today, as defined by the International Astronomical Union (IAU). These constellations cover the entire sky and help astronomers categorize and locate celestial objects. Unlike planets, constellations do not physically exist as a collection of stars in close proximity; rather, they are patterns that appear to be connected from our point of view on Earth, often many light-years apart.

Featured Constellations

Orion

Orion, visible worldwide, is one of the most recognizable constellations, known for its iconic Belt and bright stars like Betelgeuse and Rigel.

Orion

Ursa Major

Ursa Major, home to the Big Dipper, is a key constellation in the northern sky, often used for navigation.

Ursa Major

Scorpius

Scorpius, with its distinct scorpion shape, is one of the brightest constellations, featuring the red star Antares as its heart.

Scorpius

Cassiopeia

Cassiopeia is easily recognized by its "W" shape in the northern sky. Named after the vain queen in Greek mythology, it's visible year-round near Polaris.

Cassiopeia

Lyra

Lyra is home to Vega, one of the brightest stars. This small northern constellation represents Orpheus's lyre in Greek mythology, and is prominent in summer skies.

Lyra

Hercules

Hercules, named after the Roman hero, is a large northern constellation. It contains the Hercules Cluster and is visible from most locations on Earth.

Hercules

Composition

The three broad composition classes of asteroids are C-, S-, and M-types.

  • The C-type (chondrite) asteroids are most common. They probably consist of clay and silicate rocks, and are dark in appearance. They are among the most ancient objects in the solar system.
  • The S-types ("stony") are made up of silicate materials and nickel-iron.
  • The M-types are metallic (nickel-iron). The asteroids' compositional differences are related to how far from the Sun they formed. Some experienced high temperatures after they formed and partly melted, with iron sinking to the center and forcing basaltic (volcanic) lava to the surface.

The orbits of asteroids can be changed by Jupiter's massive gravity – and by occasional close encounters with Mars or other objects. These encounters can knock asteroids out of the main belt, and hurl them into space in all directions across the orbits of the other planets. Stray asteroids and asteroid fragments have slammed into Earth and the other planets in the past, playing a major role in altering the geological history of the planets and in the evolution of life on Earth.

Scientists continuously monitor Earth-crossing asteroids, whose paths intersect Earth's orbit, and near-Earth asteroids that approach Earth's orbital distance to within about 28 million miles (45 million kilometers) and may pose an impact danger. Radar is a valuable tool in detecting and monitoring potential impact hazards. By reflecting transmitted signals off objects, images and other information can be derived from the echoes. Scientists can learn a great deal about an asteroid's orbit, rotation, size, shape, and metal concentration.

Constellation Classifications

Major Constellations: These are the most well-known and easily identifiable constellations in the sky, such as Orion, Ursa Major, and Cassiopeia. These constellations have been recognized and used by humans for thousands of years for navigation and cultural significance. They form recognizable patterns and are typically located along the ecliptic or in prominent areas of the sky.

Zodiac Constellations: These 12 constellations lie along the path of the Sun across the sky. They are important in astrology and represent specific periods in the year. The zodiac includes constellations like Aries, Taurus, and Virgo, and the Sun appears to pass through each of them over the course of a year.

Celestial Sphere Constellations: These constellations are a part of the broader sky and are used by astronomers to map the night sky. They are arranged across the entire celestial sphere, not necessarily forming recognizable patterns. Examples include the constellations in the southern hemisphere, like Crux, which are not visible from the northern hemisphere.

How Constellations Get Their Names

Constellations are typically named after mythological figures, animals, or objects. Many of their names come from ancient Greek and Roman mythology. For example, the constellation of Orion is named after the Greek hunter Orion, and Ursa Major is named after the Great Bear in Greek mythology.

The International Astronomical Union (IAU) officially recognizes 88 constellations, and they are typically named based on their position in the sky or after the stars within them. For instance, Lyra is named after the lyre of Orpheus, while Scorpius is named after the scorpion from Greek mythology.

In some cases, constellations have been named after navigational aids or cultural symbols. For example, the Southern Cross (Crux) is a prominent constellation in the southern hemisphere, serving as a key navigation aid for travelers in the Southern Hemisphere.
